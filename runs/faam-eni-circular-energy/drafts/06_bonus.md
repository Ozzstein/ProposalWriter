# Section 6 — Bonus Points

## Bonus 1: Potential to Deliver Net Carbon Removals

**Not applicable.** The FAAM-ENI Circular Energy project is an LFP cathode active material manufacturing facility. The project delivers significant GHG emission avoidance through electrification of process heat and DT-driven energy optimisation (detailed in Sections 2.1 and 2.2), but it does not incorporate a mechanism for net carbon removal (negative emissions).

LFP CAM production is a materials synthesis process that consumes lithium carbonate, iron phosphate, and glucose as inputs. While glucose pyrolysis during calcination produces a carbon coating on LFP particles, this carbon originates from a biogenic source and represents a negligible mass fraction (1.5-3.0 wt% of final product [CLM-009]) that is ultimately released at end-of-life recycling — not permanently sequestered. No carbon capture and storage (CCS), direct air capture (DAC), or bioenergy with CCS (BECCS) component is included in the project scope.

The project's climate contribution is through **emission avoidance** (displacing gas-fired Chinese LFP CAM production with electrified EU production), not through net carbon removal.

## Bonus 2: SME-Coordinated and Implemented Project

**Not applicable.** The project consortium consists of:

- **FIB (FAAM)** — Coordinator. FIB is a subsidiary within the FAAM group, a large enterprise exceeding the EU SME thresholds (Commission Recommendation 2003/361) for headcount, turnover, and balance sheet total.
- **Eni S.p.A.** — Beneficiary. Eni is a multinational energy corporation and one of Italy's largest companies, with approximately 32,000 employees and annual revenues exceeding EUR 90 billion.

Neither consortium partner qualifies as an SME. This bonus criterion requires that **all** beneficiaries and affiliated entities be SMEs, which is not the case.

## Bonus 3: Maritime Sector Projects

**Not applicable.** The FAAM-ENI Circular Energy project manufactures LFP cathode active material for the general battery market (electric vehicles, grid-scale energy storage). While LFP batteries have emerging applications in maritime electrification (e.g., short-sea shipping, port equipment), the Brindisi plant's primary market and production targets are not specifically directed at maritime sector decarbonisation. The project does not meet the requirement for demonstrated potential specifically for decarbonising the maritime sector.

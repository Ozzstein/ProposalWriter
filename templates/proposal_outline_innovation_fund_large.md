# Innovation Fund — Large-Scale Projects (Grant > €7.5M)
# Application Template

> **IMPORTANT**: This is a reference template based on the standard Innovation Fund large-scale
> application structure. If the user has uploaded an official template for the specific call,
> use that instead — it takes precedence over this file.
> Check `runs/{project}/inputs/call_template.*` first.
>
> The Innovation Fund uses a **two-stage process**:
> - Stage 1: Concept Note (~25 pages) — used to pre-screen projects
> - Stage 2: Full Application (~200 pages total) — invited applicants only
>
> This template covers both stages.

## Overview

| Item | Details |
|---|---|
| Managed by | CINEA (European Climate Infrastructure and Environment Executive Agency) |
| Eligibility | Projects in EU Member States or EEA/EFTA countries demonstrating significant GHG emission reductions |
| Scope | Innovative low-carbon technologies: energy-intensive industries, renewable energy, energy storage, CCS/CCU, innovative fuels |
| Minimum grant | >€7.5M (large-scale) |
| Max funding rate | Up to 60% of relevant costs (capital and operating cost increments) |
| Submission portal | IF portal (separate from EU Funding & Tenders Portal) |

---

## Evaluation Criteria and Weights

| Criterion | Weight | Threshold |
|---|---|---|
| Degree of innovation | 25% | Pass/fail |
| Carbon abatement potential | 27.5% | Pass/fail |
| Scalability and replicability | 15% | Pass/fail |
| Project maturity | 22.5% | Pass/fail |
| Commercial viability | 10% | Pass/fail |

> ⚠️ Weights and thresholds may vary by call. Always verify in the official call document.

## Stage 1: Concept Note

*Page limit: typically 25 pages (verify per call)*

**CN-1. Project overview**
- Project title, acronym, location (country, region, site)
- Technology category and sector
- Total project cost and requested grant amount
- Expected start and end date
- Project summary (1 page max)

**CN-2. Innovative nature**
- Description of the technology
- Current TRL and target TRL at project end
- Why this is innovative compared to commercially available alternatives
- Key technical risks and mitigation

**CN-3. Carbon abatement potential**
- Estimated GHG emission reductions (tCO₂eq/year and over project lifetime)
- Methodology used for the estimate
- Baseline scenario (what would happen without the project)

**CN-4. Project maturity**
- Current project status (permits, site, financing)
- Key milestones achieved to date
- Expected financial close date
- Readiness for construction/deployment

**CN-5. Commercial viability**
- Brief market analysis
- Revenue model overview
- Financing structure (equity, debt, grant)

## Stage 2: Full Application

### Section 1 — Degree of Innovation
*Demonstrates that the technology is genuinely innovative and not commercially available at scale*

**1.1 Technology description**
- Detailed technical description of the innovation
- Process flow diagram / system architecture
- Key components and their status (commercial, prototype, novel)
- Comparison to best available technology (BAT) — quantitative where possible

**1.2 Innovation beyond the state of the art**
- Specific technical novelties (with evidence)
- Patent landscape: existing patents, your IP position
- What makes this approach different from prior art
- References to scientific/technical literature supporting the innovation claim

**1.3 Technical readiness and risk**
- Current TRL and justification
- TRL at project completion
- Technical risks table: risk, likelihood, impact, mitigation
- Results from pilot/demo phases (if available)
- Independent technical assessments or third-party validation (if available)

**1.4 Environmental impact**
- Life-cycle analysis or equivalent (carbon footprint of the technology itself)
- Do No Significant Harm (DNSH) assessment:
  - Climate change mitigation ✓ (core criterion)
  - Climate change adaptation
  - Sustainable use of water and marine resources
  - Circular economy
  - Pollution prevention
  - Biodiversity and ecosystems
- Environmental permits required and status

---

### Section 2 — Commercial Viability
*Demonstrates that the project is financially viable and can exit public support*

**2.1 Market analysis**
- Target market definition and size
- Demand drivers and market trends
- Competitive landscape
- Regulatory environment and market enablers/barriers
- Customer analysis and offtake agreements (if any)

**2.2 Revenue model and business economics**
- Revenue streams (product sales, capacity payments, carbon credits, etc.)
- Pricing assumptions and their basis
- Cost structure: CAPEX and OPEX breakdown
- Key economic metrics: LCOE/LCOP, cost per tonne CO₂ avoided
- Sensitivity analysis on key assumptions (carbon price, energy price, CAPEX)

**2.3 Financing plan**
- Total project cost (broken down: CAPEX, OPEX, contingency)
- Financing structure: equity, senior debt, junior debt, grant
- Sources of equity (committed vs. indicative)
- Debt financing status (term sheet, mandate letter, or indicative)
- Financial close date
- Financial model summary (key inputs and outputs)

**2.4 Exit from public support**
- Path to commercial operation without Innovation Fund support
- Timeline for market competitiveness (unsubsidised)
- Comparable projects achieving commercial scale (if any)

---

### Section 3 — Scalability and Replicability
*Demonstrates potential to scale up and replicate across the EU/EEA*

**3.1 Replication potential**
- Number of comparable sites/facilities across Europe where this technology could be deployed
- Market addressable by replication (GW, Mt CO₂/year, etc.)
- Barriers to replication and how they will be addressed

**3.2 Scale-up potential**
- Roadmap from current project size to commercial-scale deployment
- What would need to change (cost reduction, supply chain, regulation)
- Cost reduction trajectory (learning rate, economies of scale)

**3.3 Knowledge and technology diffusion**
- IP strategy: what will be protected, what will be shared
- Dissemination activities (publications, conferences, open data)
- Engagement with standards bodies or regulatory processes
- Replication support activities (e.g., developer workshops, toolkits)

---

### Section 4 — Carbon Abatement Potential
*The primary scoring criterion — must be rigorous and auditable*

**4.1 Baseline scenario**
- Definition of the baseline (business-as-usual without the project)
- Baseline emissions (tCO₂eq/year) with methodology and sources
- Counterfactual: what technology/process would be used instead

**4.2 GHG emission reduction methodology**
- Emission reduction calculation methodology (aligned with EU standards where applicable)
- Direct emissions reduced (Scope 1)
- Indirect emissions (Scope 2, Scope 3) — how treated
- Emissions from the project itself (deducted from reductions)
- Net annual GHG reduction (tCO₂eq/year)
- Total GHG reduction over project lifetime (tCO₂eq)

**4.3 Additionality**
- Why the project would NOT happen without Innovation Fund support (the financial gap)
- Evidence of the financing gap (IRR without vs. with grant)
- Demonstration that the grant does not result in overcompensation

**4.4 Monitoring and verification**
- Monitoring plan for GHG reductions during operation
- Data sources and measurement protocols
- Third-party verification arrangements

**4.5 Sensitivity analysis**
- Sensitivity of GHG reduction estimates to key assumptions (capacity factor, grid emission factor, feedstock, etc.)
- Low/central/high scenarios

---

### Section 5 — Project Maturity and Implementation Capacity
*Demonstrates the project can be delivered on time and within budget*

**5.1 Site and permits**
- Project site description and ownership/access status
- Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) status
- Grid connection or infrastructure connection status
- Other permits required and their current status (with expected dates)
- Zoning and land-use approvals

**5.2 Technical maturity**
- Detailed engineering status (pre-FEED, FEED, detailed design)
- Equipment procurement status (long-lead items)
- Technology provider agreements (if applicable)

**5.3 Project team and organisational capacity**
- Project developer / applicant profile and track record
- Key team members and relevant experience
- Consortium / JV structure (if applicable)
- EPC contractor strategy (selected, shortlisted, or open tender)
- O&M strategy

**5.4 Procurement strategy**
- Contracting strategy (EPC, EPCM, split contracts)
- Procurement timeline
- Supply chain analysis: key risks and mitigation

**5.5 Project schedule**
- Detailed project schedule (Gantt chart)
- Key milestones: financial close, construction start, commissioning, commercial operation
- Critical path and schedule risks

**5.6 Risk management**
- Comprehensive risk register: construction, technology, market, regulatory, financing
- For each risk: description, likelihood, impact, owner, mitigation, residual risk
- Contingency provisions in budget

---

### Section 6 — Cross-Cutting Criteria

**6.1 Environmental and social responsibility**
- Environmental management plan
- Social impact assessment
- Community engagement activities
- Just Transition considerations (if in coal/carbon-intensive region)

**6.2 Gender equality and diversity**
- Gender balance in project team and governance
- Gender equality plan (mandatory for organisations >250 employees)

**6.3 State aid compliance**
- Confirmation of compatibility with EU State Aid rules
- Notification status (if required)
- Cumulation with other public support

## Key Tips for IF Evaluators

- **Carbon abatement is the primary criterion** (27.5%): numbers must be rigorous, conservative, and methodology must be transparent. Inflated estimates are heavily penalised.
- **Additionality is critical**: evaluators check that the grant is necessary and not a windfall. Show the IRR gap clearly.
- **DNSH is mandatory**: every environmental dimension must be addressed or explicitly justified as not applicable.
- **Project maturity matters** (22.5%): projects without EIA initiation and site secured score poorly. Advanced permitting is a significant differentiator.
- **Commercial viability** (10%): show that the project has a credible path to unsubsidised operation. Pure grant-dependency is a red flag.
